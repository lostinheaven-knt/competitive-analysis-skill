# Competitor Identification Methods

Use this file when building the initial candidate pool.

## Method families

### 1. Qualitative discovery
Use for early exploration or sparse-data markets.
- expert interviews
- customer interviews or surveys
- sales and support feedback
- channel observation
- product walkthroughs and buying journey comparison

Best for:
- discovering hidden or niche competitors
- understanding how buyers actually frame alternatives

### 2. Quantitative discovery
Use when public or purchased data exists.
- market share lists
- search trend comparisons
- web traffic or app visibility
- e-commerce ranking and pricing checks
- share-of-voice or social attention
- feature overlap matrices

Best for:
- broad market scanning
- initial ranking support
- spotting strong incumbents and fast risers

### 3. Hybrid discovery
Use in most real tasks.
Typical flow:
1. generate names from search, reports, directories, and known players
2. add names mentioned by users, experts, sales, or reviews
3. filter by overlap in customer, product, and buying context
4. score or sort only after the pool is reasonably clean

## Useful heuristics
A candidate is more likely to be a real direct competitor when it overlaps on:
- same user problem
- same buyer persona
- similar product category or workflow
- similar pricing or commercial motion
- similar acquisition channel or sales motion

## Common traps
- confusing substitutes with direct competitors
- picking famous brands with little real overlap
- ignoring regional specialists
- over-relying on social buzz in place of buying overlap

## Output of this step
Produce a candidate pool where each name has:
- candidate name
- tentative type
- why it is included
- at least one evidence note

Do not jump straight from discovery to a hard shortlist without filtering.
